Our lab does research in human-centered design, human-computer/machine interaction, personal informatics, ubiquitous computing, and social computing. Our high-level goal is to develop interactive technologies that promote personal and planetary welfare. The group's research style blends creativity, innovation, and critical reflection with practical application and translational implementation.

We undertake projects that focus on advancing various aspects of this process, to:
(a) increase our fundamental scientific understanding about human behaviors and lived experiences with respect to a given domain of interest,
(b) use such insights to inform the design of novel systems that aim to improve targeted outcomes, and
(c) build and deploy these tools to study the impact in real-world settings and scenarios.

This iterative, “full stack” strategy enhances our ability to assess the merit, feasibility, and efficacy of an envisioned system, while foregrounding a deep empathy for the role of technology in a given context. This also means our research is inherently interdisciplinary, integrating perspectives and techniques from both technical and humanistic fields. In these ways, we strive to make empirical, methodological, technical, and theoretical contributions.

Overall, our projects span multiple domains, methods, people, contexts, and interaction paradigms. While not an exhaustive set, each project is tagged to reflect what problems it targets and which sociotechnical approaches we employ to develop solutions, as well as who might use or be impacted by such engineered technologies, where that technology would be used, and how people will interact with and through the technology.

Biophilic Interfaces well-being sustainability indoor spaces nature
2018 –
Nature experiences are incredibly beneficial to human health, yet our lives are increasingly mediated by digital interfaces associated with various maladies. This project explores how computational systems might instantiate biophilic design patterns and how the technologies we regularly interact with could better satisfy people's inherent need to connect with nature, promote personal restoration and environmental concern, and generally support user wellness and even flourishing.

Recruiting:
Future work will involve fabricating and implementing a number of physical and digital biophilic design concepts; however, such steps are currently on hold while COVID-19 impedes our ability to work in the lab or deploy artifacts in real environments. Feel free to get in touch if you are interested in such opportunities. Presently, we are focused on using computer vision and machine learning to examine properties of natural materials (e.g., wood grains) and biotic elements (e.g., the sound of wind), to better understand patterns in nature (e.g., the fractal geometries of a snowflake) from a mathematical, potentially computationally-reproducible point of view. Such understanding would have not only immense scientific value but could also inform the design of biophilic interfaces that embed such characteristics in ways that enhance human interaction and wellness.
Recommended background:

Families in Cars education early development children families cars
2018–
This project focuses on studying family routines and designing family-centered technologies, with a focus on scaffolding everyday and intrinsically motivated parent-child interactions around knowledge sharing and conceptual learning. Well-Building well-being sustainability built environment
2017–
People in countries like the U.S. now spend 87% of their time indoors, with research increasingly indicating that building features (e.g., lighting, materials, iconography) can substantially impact human attitudes, behaviors, and health. As part of the Hybrid Physical-Digital Spaces (HPDS) group, this research has several thrusts to develop technology that blends digital and physical infrastructure to support various cognitive, physiological, psychological, and social dimensions of occupant wellness.

Tempus Sensus time well-being clocks
2015–
Studies indicate that the experience of time is linked to well-being. In this project, we examine how physical and digital environments impact sense of time and, in turn, psychological and physiological outcomes. We are especially interested in how different built spaces impact time perceptions and how computer interfaces convey time. Based on such insights, we are designing novel digital representations and tangible materializations of time, to explore new ways of making sense of ourselves and our temporal experiences.

Recruiting:
We are currently seeking students to assist with a study aimed at better understanding dominant and emerging representations of time found in modern computer software. We are especially interested in how those representations compare to depictions of time found throughout human history (e.g., including water clocks, sundials, etc.) as well as people's experiences with those temporal archetypes. Based on insights, we are also looking to identify and pursue design opportunities for novel temporal interfaces to enhance personal well-being.
Recommended background:

- To help conduct the study, students should have experience in administering surveys, conducting interviews, and qualitative data analysis.
- For design work to envision and prototype novel temporal interfaces, students should have experience with iterative design, prototyping, and user testing.

Recruiting:
We are seeking students to implement tangible and virtual/augmented-reality (VR/AR) interfaces that support the expression and rehabilitation of various psychosomatic symptoms. Our current focus is on 1) toys (e.g., stuffed animals) that embed pressure-sensitive piezoelectric materials to sense and sooth distress in pediatric contexts, as well as 2) VR/AR interventions (e.g., embodied painting, dancing, music-making) that combine physical movement with self-expressive creative activities and art therapy, which can have powerful physiological and neurological effects, in order to support recovery and maintenance phases of rehabilitation for conditions like low-back pain and osteoarthritis.

- Prior background working with vulnerable populations (e.g., children, older adults, patients in physical therapy) is a plus.
